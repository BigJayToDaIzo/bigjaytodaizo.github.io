# Happy Monday everyone!  

So I will confess that the title is click bait.  I didn't learn a whole new science today.  What I will confirm for you now is that learning to become a Computer Scientists is only 'syllabus week' material.  Once you hit the real world do you think that you are going to be programming about programming?  Nah, you're going to go to work for a company that more than likely requires you to learn a whole new field and THEN how to improve that field with a computer program that you and your team write.

Example: I write software for the [Astronomical Society of the Pacific](https://www.astrosociety.org).  Currently working on a NASA grant to write [Astrometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrometry) software.  Guess what I get to learn a shitload about in order to write the software?  You are a good guesser! (Assuming you guessed Astrometry/Astronomy)

Todays challenge:  Figure out WTF an [annulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annulus_(mathematics)) is, and how to calculate/emulate it with Java and ImageJ!

I have 2 Ph.D. Astrophysicists in my Edwardsville office, and half a dozen more spread out around the country whom I can ask for help with my project.  Today was my boss lady [Dr. Pamela Gay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pamela_L._Gay).  We had some slack chat where she attempted to help me understand what an annulus is, and how to calculate it based on a [CCD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge-coupled_device) image.

Prior to my conversation with Pamela (she insists I call her that) I assumed that the object in the sky was the same size as the number of pixels in the image it occupied.  Of course if you give it some thought and imagine how far away any object outside of the solar system is (or even some of the objects IN it) you would pretty quickly realize that the object is far smaller than a single pixel.  The photon stream from the distant stellar object is 'disturbed' by loads of different things, but primarily the atmosphere slightly changing the direction of the photon stream about a trillion times. (which is how it takes up dozens of pixels in the image)

I didn't write this post to give a detailed account of what I learned today.  I wrote it to help software engineer hopefuls understand something and that something is this:

You will NEVER stop learning in this field.  Between changing technologies like hardware, Integrated Developer Environments, API's, language updates, and whatever field it is you're programming for, you will find that learning something new every single day isn't a stretch of the imagination at all.  You will LITERALLY learn new things almost daily.  So get it OUT OF YOUR HEAD that you know it all, or even a small potion of it.  [You know NOTHING Jon Snow!](https://www.hbo.com/game-of-thrones)

I hope your summer is going along SMASHINGLY so far and continues to do so until the very end.  Next time you get a chance, sneak outside when it's super dark and just stare up!  Find the moon (if it's up and not new), try to find Polaris, Venus, Mars, the ISS, the Big Dipper and anything else you can think of.  Install an app on your phone to help you find it!  If it points down into the ground, it's on the other side of the planet. ;)

TLDR; Keep looking up!

All my best!
